Spotter's Field Manual

A pocket briefing for the little round radar on your desk. What every page shows, what each colour, triangle and number means, and how to drive it like you built it. Keep it by the device and glance across whenever the sky gets interesting.

00Orientation

What this thing actually is

It is a standalone desk instrument that watches the real sky around you and around Munich Airport. There is no phone app and no companion server — the ESP32 microcontroller talks straight to free public flight and weather feeds over your Wi‑Fi, and paints the result on a 240 × 240 round screen. Three feeds do all the work:

FeedGives youHow often
adsb.lolLive aircraft positions (ADS‑B) around home & MUCevery 8 s
adsbdbRoute (origin → destination) & airline for tracked callsignson demand
aviationweather.govDecoded Munich (EDDM) METAR weatherevery 5 min

Between the 8‑second position updates the device gently dead‑reckons — it nudges each aircraft forward along its own heading and speed so motion looks continuous instead of teleporting. Nothing you see is faked; it is just smoothing the gap between refreshes.

01Controls

Cycling the pages & reading the clock

Left to itself, the device runs an auto‑carousel: it dwells on each page for a set time, then slides to the next. One button changes that.

THE BUTTON

short press → jump to the next page and freeze there. The carousel stops so you can stare as long as you like.

hold ≈1.5 s → resume the automatic carousel from wherever you are.

It is the onboard button on the microcontroller. Presses are caught even while the device is mid‑download, so it never feels stuck.

AUTO DWELL TIMES

Each page stays up for a tuned interval, longest where there is most to read:

Home Radar — 26 s

MUC Map — 17 s

Traffic Brief — 16 s

Nearest — 22 s · Coolest — 22 s

MUC Weather — 13 s

Interrupts: the device can override the carousel on its own. An emergency squawk (7700/7600/7500) or a very rare aircraft (coolness score ≥ 95) yanks the display straight to the traffic/coolest page so you never miss it.
02The Six Pages

Page by page

The carousel order is fixed. Here is exactly what each screen is telling you.

26 s
1

HOME RADAR

north‑up scope, centred on you

Your world from above, north at the top, your position at the crosshair. Every aircraft is drawn where it actually is relative to you.

  • Range rings quietly tour 20 / 40 / 60 km — the label at the bottom (e.g. 40 km) tells you the current outer ring. Busier skies favour a tighter zoom so labels stay readable.
  • Counters: white total under the red north arrow, red departures on the west side, green arrivals on the east.
  • Green / red / amber triangles = arriving‑at‑MUC / departing‑MUC / other traffic. Each points along its heading.
  • Faint blue rim dot = an aircraft beyond the drawn ring — direction only, deliberately dim so a ring of distant traffic doesn't shout.
  • The small grey dot dead‑centre is you (the receiver).
17 s
2

MUC MAP

airport operations view

Munich Airport drawn as a schematic — the two real parallel runways, 08L/26R and 08R/26L, at true geometry.

  • Header: the MUC title, then live temperature + wind pulled from the METAR, then the DEP / GND / ARR counters along the bottom.
  • Scale trick: airborne traffic is mapped on a fixed 20 km view (contacts from 20–40 km become blue rim markers); aircraft actually on the field are drawn at runway scale so ground movements spread out realistically.
  • Yellow slab = an aircraft physically on a runway. A bigger slab means a heavier jet.
  • Purple dot = on the ground / taxiing.
  • Labels ride on the next arrival and next departure: callsign + fr XXX / to XXX, then the arrival's ETA or the departure's distance out.
16 s
3

TRFC

the traffic brief

A one‑glance summary of the four aircraft worth knowing about right now, plus a mood‑gauge of how busy the wider sky is.

  • NEAR — the closest airborne aircraft to you.
  • COOL — the best‑scored special right now (rare type, military, government…).
  • HELI — the nearest helicopter, with its operator role if known (police, EMS…).
  • EMG — anything squawking an emergency code. Usually "— none —", and that's good.
  • The footer counts every valid aircraft within 100 km of you — a quick read on whether the sky is dead or heaving.
22 s
4

NEAREST

tracking card · closest plane

The full data card for whatever is closest overhead, refreshed every second so the numbers breathe.

  • Callsign big at the top, then type + airline in the airline's brand colour.
  • Route as ICAO codes and full city names, plus a note if it's a Munich arrival or departure.
  • Live ALT (altitude, ft), SPD (ground speed, kt), DST (distance to you, km).
  • A climb trend in metres‑per‑minute and the CPA — "passes 2.1 km from you in 1m36s", the closest it will get.
  • The red rim blip points along the bearing from you to the plane. Face it, look up — that's where it is.
22 s
5

COOLEST

tracking card · best score

Same card format as Nearest, but locked onto the most interesting aircraft in range rather than the closest — with a one‑line reason it made the cut.

  • Picks the highest coolness score in the sky: superjumbos, military transports, government flights, emergencies and more (full table below).
  • The why‑line spells out the reason — e.g. A400M ATLAS, QUEEN B747, FIGHTER JET.
  • Anything scoring ≥ 95 interrupts the carousel and jumps straight here, once per callsign, so a genuine rarity can't slip past.
  • Same red rim bearing blip — turn and look.
13 s
6

MUC WX

decoded Munich weather

The airport's official METAR observation, decoded into plain rows instead of the raw code — refreshed every 5 minutes.

  • WIND direction + speed, VIS visibility, CLOUD lowest reported layer & base, TEMP temperature/dewpoint, QNH the altimeter pressure setting.
  • A WX row flags significant weather (rain, snow, fog…) — "DRY" when the sky is clean.
  • A best‑guess active runway from the wind (e.g. landing on 26 into a westerly).
  • The flight‑category pill summarises flying conditions: VFR (green, clear) through MVFR and IFR down to LIFR (red, very low). See the glossary.
  • The tiny grey line at the bottom is the raw METAR for purists.

04Why that plane?

The coolness score

The Coolest page and the star symbol are driven by a scoring system. Aircraft type and callsign each earn a score; the highest wins. ≥ 70 earns a star; ≥ 95 interrupts the carousel. A few "live situation" bonuses can also promote an ordinary plane doing something interesting.

05Spotter's dictionary

Terms & jargon

ADS‑B
Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast. Aircraft broadcast their own GPS position, altitude and ID; this device reads a free network of receivers, not a radar dish.
Callsign
The flight's radio ID, e.g. DLH4AB. The first three letters are the airline (DLH = Lufthansa). Not the same as the tail/registration.
Squawk
A 4‑digit transponder code. Most are routine; 7500/7600/7700 are the emergency codes.
METAR
A coded airport weather observation, reissued about every 30 minutes. This device decodes Munich's into plain rows.
QNH
The altimeter pressure setting (hPa) that makes an altimeter read height above sea level. Rising QNH ≈ improving weather.
Ceiling
Height of the lowest broken/overcast cloud layer — the "lid" pilots care about.
Visibility
How far you can see horizontally; "9999" or "10 km" means 10 km or more.
VFR / MVFR / IFR / LIFR
Flight categories by ceiling & visibility, best to worst: Visual, Marginal Visual, Instrument, Low Instrument. Colour‑coded green → red.
ICAO type code
A compact aircraft‑model code, e.g. A20N = Airbus A320neo, B77W = Boeing 777‑300ER.
IATA / ICAO airport
Two naming systems: MUC (IATA, 3‑letter) and EDDM (ICAO, 4‑letter) are the same airport.
Heading / Track
Heading = where the nose points; track = the path actually made good over the ground. The triangles point along track.
Ground speed (kt)
Speed over the ground in knots (1 kt ≈ 1.85 km/h).
Altitude (ft)
Height in feet; airliners cruise around 30 000–40 000 ft.
Vertical rate
Climb/descent speed. Shown here as a climb trend in metres per minute (mpm).
CPA
Closest Point of Approach — how near a plane will pass you, and when, if both of you hold course.
Runway 08L / 26R
Numbers are the runway's magnetic heading ÷ 10 (08 ≈ 080°); the same strip is 26 from the other end. L/R distinguishes parallel runways.
Dead reckoning
Estimating a position between real updates by projecting last known speed & heading forward — how the traffic keeps moving between 8‑second fetches.
Bearing
The compass direction from you to a target. The red rim blip on tracking pages is a live bearing.
