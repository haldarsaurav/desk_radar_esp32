1 · What you are looking atTHE BIG PICTURE

The device listens to ADS-B — the position broadcasts every modern aircraft transmits about once per second — via the free adsb.lol network of hobbyist receivers. Every 8 seconds it downloads all aircraft within 100 km of your configured home position, enriches interesting ones with routes and airline names from adsbdb, pulls the official EDDM METAR weather report from aviationweather.gov, and renders it all on a 240×240 round display. Between downloads, every aircraft keeps moving by dead reckoning — its last known speed and track are used to extrapolate the position, so motion looks continuous rather than jumping every 8 seconds.

3 · Page 1 — HOME RADARYOUR PERSONAL SCOPE

North-up scope centred on your home. Up to three aircraft get stacked labels (callsign · type · altitude · speed) in thin green boxes, tethered to their triangles.

The scope tours three zoom levels — 20, 40 and 60 km — spending the most time on whichever level suits the current traffic density. The dim green number at the south position always tells you the current visible range; the dashed rings are at 1/3 and 2/3 of it.

The cyan dot in the centre is you. Everything is drawn relative to that point, north up. The three compass counters are the page's instrument cluster: total contacts under the north arrow, departures at the west point (red), arrivals at the east point (green).

Aircraft beyond the current zoom stay visible as dark-blue rim dots — you always know something is out there and in which direction, without the scope getting crowded.

The whole page uses flicker-free rendering: each frame only erases the exact pixels aircraft occupied (blip system) and skips repaints entirely when no aircraft has moved a pixel.

4 · Page 2 — MUC MAPAIRPORT OPERATIONS

The operations map. Runways at true geometry and heading (082°/262°), airborne traffic on a 20 km scope, ground traffic spread along the real field.

This page uses two projections at once: the runway schematic and everything on the field are drawn at 22 px/km so the parallel-runway layout is readable, while airborne traffic is mapped at a fixed 20 km view radius. Aircraft between 20 and 40 km out become dark-blue rim markers; beyond 40 km they are not drawn (but still count).

The next arrival and next departure each carry a three-line label: callsign + where from / where to, ARR with ETA in minutes (or DEP with distance), and current altitude + speed. The pick is latched — one arrival is followed from far out all the way to touchdown, one departure from taxi until it clears the view, before the next candidate takes over. Watch a whole approach happen.

Counters below the header: departures · on the ground · arrivals. MUC's two runways are 08L/26R (north) and 08R/26L (south) — 4000 m of concrete each; the number is the magnetic heading ÷ 10, the letter distinguishes the parallel pair.

5 · Pages 3–5 — TRFC, NEAREST, COOLESTTHE BRIEFING & THE CARDS

TRFC — the 10-second briefing

Four rows answer the spotter's first questions: NEAR — the closest airborne aircraft; COOL — the most interesting one (star logic, §6); HELI — the nearest helicopter, with its operator role decoded from the callsign; EMG — any emergency squawk. The big number at the bottom is the total aircraft count within 100 km of you.

Search ranges: NEAR and COOL pick within 60 km, HELI within 45 km, EMG within 60 km — all tunable in config.h.

NEAREST & COOLEST — the data cards

One aircraft, big numbers: callsign (amber), type + airline in the carrier's brand colour (Lufthansa yellow, Ryanair navy...), route as codes and full city names, then live ALT / SPD / DST, the climb trend in metres per minute, and the line that makes the page: CPA.

CPA — closest point of approach. "CPA 2.1km in 1m36s" means: if this aircraft keeps its current track and speed, its closest pass to your position will be 2.1 km away, 96 seconds from now. When the CPA is small — go outside. The numbers tick live every second between data updates.

The red rim blip sits on the compass bearing from you to the aircraft — it is literally the direction to face. COOLEST adds a why-line ("emergency squawk", "Munich arrival", "C-17 GLOBEMASTER"...) and any aircraft scoring ≥ 95 interrupts the page carousel immediately.

The status pill is the standard flight category: VFR MVFR IFR LIFR — computed from visibility and ceiling, exactly like the coloured dots on US aviation weather maps. The raw METAR text at the bottom is the unfiltered original for anyone who reads METAR for fun.

8 · Terms a spotter should ownGLOSSARY

TermMeaning
ADS-BAutomatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast. Aircraft self-report GPS position, altitude, speed, track and callsign on 1090 MHz, unencrypted. Community receivers feed networks like adsb.lol.
CallsignWhat the crew uses on the radio: airline ICAO code + flight identifier (DLH4RV = Lufthansa). Private aircraft often broadcast their registration instead (DEODY = D-EODY, a German GA aircraft; HB… = Switzerland, OE… = Austria).
Type codeICAO airframe designator: A321, B77W (777-300ER), A359 (A350-900), PA18 (Piper Super Cub), C172… The device translates common ones to full names on the cards.
Squawk4-digit transponder code assigned by ATC. Three are emergencies: 7700 general emergency, 7600 radio failure, 7500 unlawful interference.
ETA "ARR 2m"Estimated minutes until the aircraft reaches the airport, from its live distance and ground speed.
Ground speedSpeed over the ground (what ADS-B reports) — differs from airspeed by the wind. All speeds shown in km/h, altitudes in metres, climb rates in m/min.
Dead reckoningEstimating position between data updates from last known speed + track. Used for all between-fetch motion.
HeavyWake-turbulence category for big aircraft (B777, A350, B747, A380 "Super"). Bigger yellow marker on the runway.
08L / 26RRunway names: magnetic heading ÷ 10, so the same concrete is 08 (082°, eastbound ops) one way and 26 (262°, westbound) the other. L/R distinguishes the parallel pair. Munich is westbound (26) most of the time.

9 · Living with itCONTROLS & CARE

ActionEffect
Short pressNext page — and the carousel pauses on it
Long press (1.2 s)Resume automatic page rotation
Power onRadar ping + fly-through animation, always lands on the HOME RADAR page
BOOT button quirk: the default page button is the ESP32's BOOT button (GPIO9). Never hold it while plugging in USB — that enters the serial bootloader. Release and re-plug if the screen stays black.

The firmware is built to run unattended forever: it re-associates Wi-Fi after repeated fetch failures, restarts itself if the heap ever runs low or the network stays dead, and does one preventive restart per day at a quiet moment (you will at most notice the 10-second boot animation). Failed data fetches show a small "retry" pill on the radar rather than freezing anything, and every timer is rollover-safe.

All aircraft data comes from volunteer receivers — coverage at low altitude depends on who is feeding nearby, so a plane on very short final can occasionally blink out before touchdown. That is the network, not the device.
