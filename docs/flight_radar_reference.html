Radar Frame

20 / 40 / 60 km Main radar rings in the current 60 km scope.

Grid lines Bearing cross and inner range rings.

White total Current visible traffic count.

Red count MUC departures.

Green count MUC arrivals.

The colors are display hints, not official ATC status. Arrival and departure are inferred from route, distance to MUC, altitude, vertical speed, and whether the track is moving toward or away from the airport.

Main radar labels are banded: the firmware prefers one detail stack from 0-20 km, one from 20-40 km, and one from 40-60 km. If a band is empty, it fills the slot with the next best aircraft that still fits.

Flight Radar Parameters

Field Meaning Unit / Example
CALLSIGN Radio or airline flight identifier transmitted by the aircraft. DLH3CH, AUA18Y
ROUTE Origin and destination airport codes when a route lookup is available. VIE > MUC
DIST Distance from your configured receiver/home position to the aircraft. km
BRG Bearing from you to the aircraft. The letter is the compass direction. 126 SE
ALT Aircraft altitude converted for the display. Low values near MUC usually mean approach, climbout, or ground traffic. m
V/S Vertical speed. Negative means descending, positive means climbing. m/s
SPD Ground speed from ADS-B data. km/h
TRK Track over ground. This is where the aircraft is moving, not necessarily where the nose points in wind. degrees plus compass letter
A/C ICAO aircraft type code and decoded aircraft family when known. E195 = Embraer E195
SQK Squawk/transponder code. Special emergency codes are highlighted. 1000, 7700
CPA Closest point of approach. The tracker estimates how close this aircraft will get if it keeps its current track and speed. CPA 5.9km in 2m12s
LOOK Simple spotting direction: turn your eyes or antenna toward that compass direction. LOOK SE
GND Ground flag. The aircraft reports or appears to be on the ground. on field

Squawk Codes

What Squawk Means

A squawk is the four-digit transponder code an aircraft sends with its radar/ADS-B replies. Air traffic control assigns codes so aircraft can be identified. Some codes have standard meanings.

This radar is a spotting display only. It is not a safety, navigation, or ATC tool.

Important Codes

7700 General emergency. The firmware treats this as maximum interest and shows emergency styling.
7600 Radio communication failure.
7500 Unlawful interference / hijack alert code.
7000 Common VFR conspicuity code in much of Europe.
1000 Common Mode S conspicuity style code seen often in European airspace.

Weather Parameters

Field Meaning Common Codes
WIND Wind direction and speed in knots. Gusts may appear with G. 28005KT, VRB03KT, 29012G22KT
VIS Visibility. CAVOK means visibility and cloud/weather are good enough for the shorthand. 9999, 6000, CAVOK
CLOUD Cloud coverage and base height. The number is hundreds of feet. FEW020, SCT035, BKN012, OVC008
TEMP Air temperature at the airport. Celsius
QNH Sea-level pressure setting used by aircraft altimeters near the airport. hPa, for example 1020hPa
WX Weather phenomena. The firmware shows DRY when no active weather code is found. RA rain, SH showers, SN snow, BR mist, FG fog, TS thunderstorm
RWY Likely runway direction inferred from wind, not official ATC runway assignment. 08 L/R or 26 L/R
OPS Simple operating condition label derived from visibility, cloud base, and weather. NORMAL OPS, MARGINAL, LOW VIS OPS

METAR data is weather reporting text. The page trims it into fast-read spotting fields, then keeps the raw METAR at the bottom for checking.

Rare / Star Rules

What Gets a Star

The firmware gives aircraft a cool score. A score of 70 or higher gets the special star marker. A score of 95 or higher is treated as a real alert-worthy aircraft for the coolest tracker.

The coolest page shows the reason directly, for example: emergency squawk, Munich arrival, A380 superjumbo, or low overhead pass.

How The Coolest Plane Is Picked

Every aircraft gets one score from live behavior, aircraft type, and callsign rules. The COOLEST page follows the aircraft with the highest current score, so it can change dynamically when something more interesting appears.

Live urgency wins first: emergency squawks outrank everything, then rare military/heavy aircraft, special callsigns, low passes, Munich final arrivals, climbouts, helicopters, and other notable aircraft types.

If two aircraft are similarly interesting, the closer/currently more relevant one is usually the better display target because it is easier to spot from your desk.

Ranges And Data Caveats

Current Ranges

Main radar60 km around your configured receiver position; rings read 20, 40, and 60 km.
Activity count60 km regional count on Traffic Brief; colour changes with busyness.
MUC map60 km MUC-centred aircraft scope with a readable runway schematic in the middle.
Helicopter search45 km so rotorcraft are easier to catch.
Emergency search60 km, matching the current regional ADS-B fetch.
Route lookupUsed to color arrivals/departures when origin or destination is known.

Important Caveats

ADS-B is live-ish, not perfect. Aircraft can disappear, update late, have missing callsigns, or report incomplete altitude/route data.

Arrival/departure labels are inferred. The firmware looks at route, distance from Munich, altitude, vertical speed, movement direction, and ground state.

Weather is parsed from METAR text. The runway direction is a helpful wind-based guess, not an official airport assignment.
